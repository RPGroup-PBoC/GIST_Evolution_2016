    Welcome!

    Evolution has been called biologyâ€™s greatest idea. The objective of this course is to use specific case studies to paint a picture of modern thinking on evolution, both of living organisms and the planet they inhabit. I will begin with the study of one of the great puzzles in evolution at the time that Darwin and Wallace made their discovery of the facts and theory of evolution, namely, the whale. Darwin wondered what the transitional forms might look like and now we have answers to that question both in terms of fossils and reflected in the DNA. After introducing the great story of the whale, we will turn to a consideration of biogeography with special emphasis on amphibians found on oceanic islands. Once again, we will see how DNA sequencing has allowed us to understand deep time in evolution. We then continue with the study of evolution on oceanic islands, this time turning our attention to the Galapagos Islands and the evolution of their biota. With these important case studies in hand, having taught us about heritable variation and natural selection, we will then turn our attention to laboratory evolution with experiments on fruit flies that teach us about genetic drift and experiments on bacteria illustrating the power of selection. Both of these examples will be interpreted using mathematical descriptions of population genetics. We then turn to a systematic description of the power of single mutations in case studies ranging from antibiotic and viral resistance to coat color in mammoths. Depending upon how much time is left, we will then consider a suite of other fascinating case studies.
